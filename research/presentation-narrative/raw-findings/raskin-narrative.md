# Andy Raskin — 5-Part Strategic Narrative ("The Greatest Sales Deck")

## Sources: andyraskin.com (Official), Medium article (2016, widely cited)

### The 5-Part Narrative Structure

Raskin's framework (from "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen," originally published on Medium in 2016, one of the most-shared articles on B2B presentation strategy) analyzes Zuora's sales deck to identify 5 elements that make it exceptional:

#### 1. Name a Big, Relevant Change in the World
- **Don't** start with "who we are" or "what we do"
- **Do** start with an undeniable shift happening in the industry
- This shift should be external to your company — a market force, technology change, or behavioral shift
- The change should feel urgent and inevitable
- **Purpose:** Creates common ground and urgency without talking about yourself

#### 2. Show There Will Be Winners and Losers
- Frame the change as creating a fork in the road
- Those who adapt to the change will thrive (winners)
- Those who ignore it will fall behind (losers)
- **Purpose:** Creates stakes and motivates action — the audience doesn't want to be a loser

#### 3. Tease the Promised Land
- Paint a vivid picture of the future for "winners"
- This is NOT your product or service — it's the outcome your audience desires
- Make it desirable but don't explain HOW to get there yet
- **Purpose:** Creates aspiration and desire before introducing your solution

#### 4. Introduce Features as "Magic Gifts"
- Only NOW do you introduce your product/solution capabilities
- Frame each feature as a "magic gift" that helps the audience reach the Promised Land
- Each capability should map directly to an obstacle between current state and Promised Land
- **Purpose:** Features feel like enablers of a story, not a feature dump

#### 5. Present Evidence You Can Make the Story Come True
- Case studies, data, testimonials
- Prove that the Promised Land isn't fantasy — others have already arrived
- **Purpose:** Overcomes the "too good to be true" objection

### Raskin Framework Applied to Delta Crown

| Element | Delta Crown Application |
|---------|------------------------|
| 1. Big Change | Multi-brand beauty operations are shifting from artisanal/ad-hoc to systematized/scalable |
| 2. Winners & Losers | Brands that standardize scale profitably; those that don't stay stuck as single-location |
| 3. Promised Land | A plug-and-play operational framework where each new brand/location launches in weeks, not months |
| 4. Magic Gifts | SharePoint Hub & Spoke, Delta Crown Model™, Standardized Playbook |
| 5. Evidence | Delta Crown as the working lighthouse pilot (12.5% → proof of concept) |

### Why This Works for Seeking Approval

Unlike feature-driven presentations, this narrative:
- Makes the audience feel the **urgency** of the change (not just the features)
- Positions the solution as **inevitable**, not optional
- Moves from **external market forces** to **internal capability**, which feels more credible
- The "evidence" section is where the pilot program naturally fits
