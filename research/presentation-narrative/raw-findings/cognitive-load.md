# Cognitive Load Research for Presentation Design

## Sources: Wikipedia (Cognitive Load Theory), Sweller (1988), Miller (1956), Duarte (2012)

### Cognitive Load Theory — Foundation

**Developed by:** John Sweller, late 1980s (originally studying problem-solving)

**Core principle:** "The quality of instructional design will be raised if greater consideration is given to the role and limitations of working memory."

### Three Types of Cognitive Load

| Type | Definition | Presentation Implication |
|------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Intrinsic** | Inherent difficulty of the topic itself | Can't change the complexity of multi-brand operations, but CAN break it into digestible sub-schemas |
| **Extraneous** | Load created by HOW information is presented | This is what slide design directly controls — reduce it ruthlessly |
| **Germane** | Productive effort spent creating mental models | This is what you WANT — audience building understanding of your framework |

**Critical insight:** These three types compete for the same limited working memory resources. If extraneous load is high, germane load (actual learning/understanding) decreases.

### Miller's Law — The 7±2 Rule

George A. Miller (1956): Humans can hold **7 ± 2 items** in short-term memory at once.

**Implication for slides:**
- Maximum 5-7 bullet points per slide (ideally 3-5)
- Maximum 3-4 data comparisons per chart
- Maximum 1 key message per slide

### Chunking (Simon & Chase, 1973)

People organize information in "chunks" — grouped related items that occupy a single slot in working memory.

**Implication for presentations:**
- Group related metrics into meaningful categories
- Use visual hierarchy to signal what belongs together
- Name your chunks memorably (e.g., "The Delta Crown Model™" is a chunk label)

### The Split-Attention Effect (Chandler & Sweller, 1992)

When learners must mentally integrate multiple sources of information, cognitive load increases and learning decreases.

**Implication for slides:**
- Don't put a chart on one side and explanation on the other
- Integrate labels directly into visuals
- Don't require the audience to cross-reference between slides

### The Modality Effect (Mousavi, Low & Sweller, 1995)

Cognitive load is reduced when information is presented through BOTH auditory and visual channels simultaneously, rather than through one channel alone.

**Implication for presentations:**
- Slides should be VISUAL (charts, diagrams, images)
- Words should come from the SPEAKER (auditory)
- Don't read slides aloud — this creates redundancy that increases load

### The Expertise Reversal Effect (Kalyuga et al., 2003)

Information that helps novices can actually HURT experts. Detailed explanations that benefit beginners create extraneous load for experienced audiences.

**Critical for executive presentations:**
- Executives are EXPERTS in business decision-making
- Over-explaining business fundamentals insults their intelligence
- Detailed technical walkthroughs increase extraneous load for non-technical executives
- Provide detail in appendix/slidedoc, not in the presentation

### Effects of Heavy Cognitive Load

From the research (multiple sources):
- Heavy load creates errors and interference in task completion
- Heavy load increases reliance on pattern recognition and stereotypes
- Heavy load pushes processing into subconscious shortcuts
- **Translation:** Overloaded executives will fall back on gut reactions and pre-existing biases rather than engaging with your actual argument

### Quantitative Guidelines for Slide Design

Based on cognitive load research synthesis:

| Element | Maximum for Executives | Rationale |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|
| Words per slide | 25-40 | Minimize extraneous reading load |
| Bullet points | 3-5 | Miller's Law (chunk limit) |
| Data points per chart | 3-4 | Comparison capacity limit |
| Slides per minute | ~1 per 2-3 minutes | Processing time for schema construction |
| Total slides (20 min) | 8-10 | Allows 2-3 min per concept |
| Total slides (30 min) | 10-15 | Allows 2-3 min per concept |
| Colors per chart | 3-5 | Visual discrimination limit |
| Font sizes | 2-3 per slide | Visual hierarchy simplicity |

### The "Slidedoc" Solution (Duarte)

Nancy Duarte's concept directly addresses cognitive load:

> "Many people do brilliant thinking in PowerPoint, but project dense slides. Instead, presenters should use a 'slidedoc' for their dense data."

**The two-artifact approach:**
1. **Cinematic slides** — Minimal, visual, speaker-driven (for the presentation)
2. **Slidedocs** — Dense, readable, self-contained (for pre-read or follow-up)

This separates the intrinsic load (complex data) from the presentation moment, allowing executives to engage with the narrative during the talk and deep-dive into data on their own time.
