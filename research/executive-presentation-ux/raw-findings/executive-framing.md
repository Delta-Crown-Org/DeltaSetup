# Raw Findings: Executive Presentation Frameworks & Progress Framing

## Part A: Nancy Duarte — 11 Tips for Executive Slides (MIT Sloan, April 2024)

**Source:** MIT Sloan Management Review, Column, April 1, 2024
**Author:** Nancy Duarte, CEO of Duarte Inc.
**Reading Time:** 7 minutes
**URL:** https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/how-to-create-slides-that-suit-your-superiors-11-tips/

### Context
Duarte interviewed 20 customers in senior roles at Fortune 100 companies. The biggest pain point: figuring out what's "just right" for executive audiences. Teams often bring vast amounts of backup content "just in case."

### Four Things Every Exec's Slides MUST Have

**1. Start with an executive summary (three-act structure):**
- Act 1: State current realities
- Act 2: Clearly state the problem or opportunity and its potential impact
- Act 3: Explain how your recommendation solves the problem and proposed next steps

**2. Have a logical organization:**
- "If your audience reads only the headline of every slide, the order should be coherent and make most of the case for you"
- Content below each headline must support the title statement
- "Kill your darlings" — remove everything that doesn't support your point

**3. Make it skimmable:**
- Clear visual hierarchy
- Bold headings, bullet points, numbered lists
- Highlight key takeaways in different color or font size

**4. Focus on concise insights:**
- Every slide serves a purpose contributing to decision-making
- "Don't use 100 words when 20 words will nail it"
- Use AI tools to trim fluff if needed

### Five Preferences to Confirm With Your Audience

**5. Ask how dense is too dense:**
- Some executives prefer detailed slides with comprehensive data
- Others favor high-level approach
- Balance informative content with readability

**6. Confirm delivery format and timing:**
- Some execs like information presented TO them
- Others prefer a **pre-read followed by discussion**
- Duarte recommends "Slidedocs" — visual documents for pre-reads
- "I'm a huge fan of pre-reads and prefer to use my time in meetings to ask questions and build alignment"

**7. Find out data visualization preferences:**
- Bar charts: for exact numbers (eyes move across specifics)
- Line charts: for shape of trend over time
- Some prefer annotated charts highlighting the key point
- Others want to draw their own conclusions
- Example: One CEO wants "numbers, only in a table, and only in two colors — black and red"
- **"When the stakes are high, have a conceptual thinker help with diagrams"**

**8. Identify which details need spelling out:**
- "Do the execs all speak geek? Or do you need to explain the technology?"
- **"Eliminate internal jargon and acronyms or unpack those bits"**
- Especially critical when proposal involves expertise outside executives' domain

**9. Ask whether appendices will be useful:**
- Add supporting data as appendix
- Main slides remain focused and accessible
- Comprehensive background available for those who want it

### Two Tips for Presentation Skills

**10. Anticipate questions, practice answers:**
- Gather a small team to challenge every point
- Role-play as "rapidly inquisitive exec" or "doubting naysayer exec"

**11. Ask for feedback after the presentation:**
- Establish a feedback loop
- Talk to people who have had ideas funded about what worked

### Key Quote
> "Empathetically understanding your audience members and how they process information, whether it's executives or peers, sets up your ideas for success. Clarity creates efficiency."

## Part B: Executive Presentation Framework (Hazeldine, 2026)

**Source:** winningpresentations.com, Updated January 2026
**Author:** Mary Beth Hazeldine (25 years at JPMorgan, PwC, RBS, Commerzbank; trained 5,000+ executives)

### Quick Answer
> "Executive presentations that get results follow a specific structure: lead with your recommendation (not background), limit to 12 slides maximum, include only three supporting points per argument, and end with a clear ask."

### The Five Problems That Kill Executive Presentations

1. **Building up to the conclusion** — Academic style (evidence → conclusion) is wrong for executives. They want: recommendation → evidence.

2. **Too much content** — "Your 40-slide deck demonstrates how much work you've done. Executives don't care about your effort — they care about the decision."

3. **Presenting information instead of decisions** — "Here's an update on Project X" (information) vs. "Project X is on track. We need a decision on the vendor delay — I recommend accepting it." (decision)

4. **Weak executive summary** — "If your opening slide doesn't tell them everything they need to know in 60 seconds, you've already lost momentum."

5. **No clear ask** — "If you don't tell executives what you need, they'll assume you don't need anything."

### The Five Principles That Work

**Principle 1: Lead with your recommendation**
- Visible within 60 seconds
- Weak: "Technology Infrastructure Assessment"
- Strong: "Recommendation: Approve £1.2M Platform Upgrade — 180% ROI"

**Principle 2: Structure for scanning**
- Comprehensible even without hearing you speak
- Slide titles tell the story (not "Overview" or "Background")
- Key points visible without reading paragraphs
- Test: "Can someone understand your argument by reading only the slide titles?"

**Principle 3: Three supporting points maximum**
- "The human brain struggles to hold more than three to four items in working memory"
- "Give executives five reasons and they'll remember none. Give them three and they'll remember all of them."

**Principle 4: Anticipate objections**
- Include a risks slide with: Top 3-5 risks, likelihood/impact, mitigation strategy
- "When executives ask about risks and you already have a thoughtful answer, you build credibility."

**Principle 5: End with a clear ask**
- Weak: "We'd appreciate your guidance on next steps."
- Strong: "I need budget approval today to hit the Q3 deadline. Implementation plan is ready to execute."

### The 12-Slide Structure
1. Executive Summary — Everything in 60 seconds
2. Situation — Current state, briefly
3. Problem/Opportunity — Why action is needed
4. Recommendation — What you want them to do
5. Options Considered — Shows rigorous thinking
6. Implementation Plan — How you'll execute
7. Resource Requirements — What you need
8. Risk Assessment — What could go wrong
9. Timeline — Key milestones
10. Success Metrics — How you'll measure
11. Governance — Who's accountable
12. The Ask — Specific decision needed

### Project Status Example (Most Relevant to Delta Crown)

**What DOESN'T work:**
- Comprehensive status on all 15 workstreams
- Every milestone listed with percentage complete
- Issues mentioned but minimised
- No clear decision requested
- Result: "Executives tune out, miss the one thing that needed attention"

**What WORKS:**
- Opens with: "Project Phoenix: On track overall, need decision on vendor issue"
- Green/amber/red summary of all workstreams on ONE slide
- Deep dive only on the issue requiring decision
- Clear options presented with recommendation
- Result: "Decision made in 10 minutes, meeting ends early"

### Checklist
- ☐ Recommendation visible within 60 seconds
- ☐ Executive summary: situation, recommendation, 3 supporting points, ask
- ☐ 12 slides or fewer (excluding appendix)
- ☐ Slide titles tell the story when read in sequence
- ☐ 3 supporting points maximum per argument
- ☐ Risks addressed with mitigation
- ☐ Clear ask on final slide
- ☐ First 30 seconds memorised
- ☐ Total presentation under 20 minutes
- ☐ Appendix ready for detail questions

## Part C: McKinsey Presentation Structure

**Source:** slidemodel.com/mckinsey-presentation-structure (September 2024)

### Core Principles
1. **Pyramid Principle** (Barbara Minto): Conclusion first → Supporting arguments → Evidence/data
2. **MECE Framework**: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps
3. **Data-Driven Insights**: Every recommendation backed by quantitative or qualitative data
4. **Minimalist Design**: Clean, uncluttered, consistent
5. **Logical Flow**: Narrative guides audience through the argument
6. **Audience-Centric**: Tailored to specific audience needs

### SCR Storyline Structure
- **Situation**: Frame the core issue (current state)
- **Complication**: Identify challenges/urgency
- **Resolution**: Lead to the recommendation

### McKinsey Slide Anatomy
- **Action title**: Headline captures the takeaway (not just a label — it TELLS you what to know)
- **Body**: Charts, text, infographics — ONE type per slide
- **Footer**: Source notes (minimal)

### Slide Types in McKinsey Decks
1. **Full-Slide Graphs** — One chart, full page, minimal text. Maximum data impact.
2. **Infographics + Text** — Visuals summarize, short text provides context
3. **Full-Text Slides** — Only when necessary. Clean layout, bullet points, ample white space.
4. **Text + Icons** — Icons reduce need for lengthy descriptions. Organized into sections.

### Key Design Rules
- Same fonts, colors, layouts throughout
- Use white space to reduce visual fatigue
- Strong contrast between text and background
- Clear visual hierarchy: headings > subheadings > body
- Header (action title) → Body → Footer (source notes)

## Part D: Progress Framing Research & Strategies

### Psychological Foundations

**Framing Effect (Kahneman & Tversky):**
- Same information, different perception based on presentation
- "90% fat-free" perceived more positively than "10% fat"
- Applied: "Phase 1 complete" > "12.5% done"

**Goal Gradient Effect (Hull, 1932; Kivetz et al., 2006):**
- Motivation increases as perceived proximity to goal increases
- Break large projects into smaller goals — each completion feels closer
- A loyalty card with 2 pre-filled stamps (12 needed) outperforms a card with 0 stamps (10 needed) — same effort, different perception

**Endowed Progress Effect (Nunes & Dreze, 2006):**
- People are more motivated when they perceive existing progress
- Show what's accomplished, not just what remains

**Loss Aversion:**
- Losses feel ~2x as painful as equivalent gains
- Frame as gains (achieved) not losses (remaining)

### Practical Reframing for 12.5% Completion

| Strategy | Example | Psychological Mechanism |
|----------|---------|------------------------|
| Milestone framing | "Phase 1 of 8: Complete ✓" | Goal gradient — completed unit feels substantial |
| Velocity framing | "On schedule — tracking to Q4 delivery" | Trajectory > position |
| Deliverable framing | "3 of 4 Phase 1 deliverables shipped" | Concrete > abstract |
| Foundation narrative | "Architecture decisions (hardest part) are done" | Positions remaining work as easier |
| Risk-reduction framing | "Critical decisions made. Execution risk: Low" | Executives think in risk |
| Investment framing | "15% of budget deployed → [list of capabilities]" | Value for money |

### Visual Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- ✅ Checklist with Phase 1 checked — tangible, discrete
- ✅ RAG status indicators — executives know this language
- ✅ Milestone timeline with first milestone highlighted
- ✅ Declining risk curve showing highest-risk phase complete

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Long progress bar at 12.5% — visually emphasizes distance remaining
- ❌ Pie chart showing 12.5% vs 87.5% — makes progress look trivial
- ❌ Comparison to total scope — dwarfs early-stage work
- ❌ Language: "only," "just," or any minimizing qualifiers
- ❌ Equal visual weight for all 8 phases — grey out future phases

### Executive Status Slide Best Practice

From Deckary (2026), citing Smartsheet:
> "Executives spend less than 30 seconds per status slide"

Implication: Status must be instantly scannable. RAG + headline + one decision point.

Recommended format:
```
[Slide Title as Assertion]: "Phase 1 Complete, On Track for Q4 Full Deployment"

[RAG Summary Bar]
✅ Phase 1: Foundation     | COMPLETE
🔵 Phase 2: Migration     | ON TRACK (Week 3 of 6)
⬜ Phases 3-8              | Scheduled per plan

[Single Key Metric]
"Highest-complexity phase delivered on schedule and under budget"

[If Decision Needed]
"Decision required: Approve Phase 2 vendor selection by [date]"
```
