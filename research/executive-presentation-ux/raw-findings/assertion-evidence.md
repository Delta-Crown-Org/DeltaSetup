# Raw Findings: Assertion-Evidence Slide Design Model

## The Model

**Developer:** Michael Alley, Penn State University (Leonhard Center)
**Funding:** NSF Grant 1323230
**Website:** https://www.assertion-evidence.com
**Google Scholar Citations:** 1,917+

### Core Structure
Every slide has two elements:
1. **Assertion headline** — A complete sentence that states the key message (not a topic phrase)
2. **Visual evidence** — An image, diagram, chart, or graph that supports the assertion (not a bulleted list)

### Why Traditional Slides Fail
From assertion-evidence.com:
> "In many engineering and scientific presentations, the biggest weakness is that presenters follow PowerPoint's defaults of a phrase headline supported by a bulleted list. In short, those defaults lead to much noise. For instance, the clutter caused by PowerPoint's defaults, which were established in the 1980s, makes it difficult for the audience to know where on the slide to look when the presenter is speaking."

## Research Studies

### Study 1: Audience Comprehension (Garner & Alley, 2013)

**Full Citation:** Garner, J. & Alley, M. (2013). How the Design of Presentation Slides Affects Audience Comprehension: A Case for the Assertion-Evidence Approach. *International Journal of Engineering Education*, 29(6), 1564-1579.

**Methodology:**
- Two audiences learned the same technical subject
- Heard the EXACT SAME words (controlled variable)
- Viewed DIFFERENT slides (independent variable):
  - Group A: Traditional (topic-phrase headline + bulleted list OR bulleted list + graphic)
  - Group B: Assertion-evidence (sentence headline stating main message + visual evidence)

**Results:**
- The assertion-evidence audience **understood and remembered content better**
- The difference was **statistically significant (p < .01)**

**Implications:**
- The slide design itself — independent of what the speaker says — significantly affects comprehension
- Simply changing from bullet points to visual evidence with assertion headlines improves learning

### Study 2: Presenter Understanding (Garner, Alley & Aippersbach, 2016)

**Full Citation:** Garner, J.K., Alley, M., & Aippersbach, S. (2016). *International Journal of Engineering Education*, 32(1A). Also presented at 2013 ASEE Annual Conference.

**Research Question:** Does slide design affect how much the PRESENTER learns about the content?

**Methodology:**
- Student presenters created slides using either assertion-evidence or traditional topic-subtopic approach
- Measured presenter understanding of the content afterward

**Results:**
- Presenters using assertion-evidence **understood the content better** than those using traditional approach
- The instruction needed to teach the assertion-evidence approach was **minimal**

**Key Insight:**
> "The results of this study suggest that presenters using an assertion-evidence approach think more deeply about the content during the preparation of the slides than presenters following the typical approach."

**Implication:** The process of writing sentence-headline assertions FORCES deeper thinking about what the key message actually is. This is a design process benefit, not just a communication benefit.

### Study 3: Comprehension + Cognitive Load (Semantic Scholar Aggregation)

**Source:** semanticscholar.org — "How the design of presentation slides affects audience comprehension"

**Aggregated Findings:**
> "Findings support the use of the assertion-evidence structure for presentations in engineering education, which includes **superior comprehension** and **fewer misconceptions** for the assertion-evidence group as well as **lower perceived cognitive load**."

Three distinct benefits:
1. Better comprehension
2. Fewer misconceptions (not just "more learning" but "less wrong learning")
3. Lower cognitive load (audience feels less overwhelmed)

### Study 4: Advanced Audiences & SmartArt Warning (Wolfe et al., 2023)

**Full Citation:** Wolfe, J., Shanmugaraj, N., Reineke, J., Caton Peet, L., & Moreau, C.P. (2023). Advancing the Knowledge Base of Effective Presentation Slide Design: Three Pilot Studies. *Journal of Technical Writing and Communication* (July 2023).

**Institution:** Carnegie Mellon University

**Three Pilot Studies:**

**Pilot 1:** CTML principles can be effective for presenting advanced research to **expert audiences**
- Important: These principles work for executive-level (not just student) audiences

**Pilot 2:** Visual organizer images (like PowerPoint's SmartArt) can **BACKFIRE**
- They unintentionally indicate imprecise relationships
- They add little in terms of visual interest
- **This is directly relevant to the Delta Crown presentation** which uses Material Design cards and visual organizers

**Pilot 3:** When presenting long quotations, presenters should avoid verbatim reading
- Consider abridging or paraphrasing

## Practical Implementation Guide

### Writing Assertion Headlines

**Bad (Topic Phrases):**
- "SharePoint Architecture"
- "Cost Analysis"  
- "Implementation Timeline"
- "Security Overview"
- "Operational Efficiency"

**Good (Assertion Sentences):**
- "Hub & spoke architecture gives each brand its own workspace at zero incremental cost"
- "Unified licensing saves $47,000 annually compared to per-brand subscriptions"
- "Phase 1 foundation is complete; full deployment targets Q4 2026"
- "Single sign-on with conditional access eliminates the #1 security vulnerability"
- "Automated provisioning reduces new employee setup from 3 days to 15 minutes"

### Choosing Visual Evidence

For each assertion, the evidence should be:

| Assertion Type | Best Evidence |
|---------------|--------------|
| Cost savings | Bar chart comparing before/after, or single large number |
| Architecture concept | Simplified diagram with labeled components |
| Process improvement | Before/after comparison or flow diagram |
| Timeline/roadmap | Milestone chart (not Gantt) |
| Risk mitigation | Heat map or simple matrix |
| User experience | Screenshot or mockup |
| Performance metric | Single large number with trend arrow |

### What NOT to Use as Evidence
- ❌ Bulleted lists (they ARE the traditional approach being replaced)
- ❌ SmartArt or decorative organizers (Wolfe et al., 2023 — they backfire)
- ❌ Stock photos unrelated to the content (adds noise, not signal)
- ❌ Complex diagrams that require extensive reading (defeats the purpose)
- ❌ Tables with more than 4-5 rows (better as a chart)
