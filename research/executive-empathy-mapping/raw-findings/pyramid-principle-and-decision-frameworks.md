# Pyramid Principle & Executive Decision Frameworks — Raw Findings

## Source 1: The Pyramid Principle (WinningPresentations.com)
**URL:** https://winningpresentations.com/pyramid-principle-presentations/
**Updated:** January 2026
**Author:** Mary Beth Hazeldine (24 years at JPMorgan, PwC, RBS, Commerzbank)

### What Is It
- Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1960s
- Standard for executive communication at consulting firms, investment banks, Fortune 500
- Core idea: **Start with the answer**
- Structure: Single answer → 3 supporting points → Evidence under each

### Why It Works
1. **Executives are time-poor** — CEO may have 15 minutes between meetings
2. **Executives are decision-makers** — Need: What do you recommend? Why? What do you need from me?
3. **Executives will interrupt** — With answer stated, interruptions become productive exploration

### The Structure (Slide by Slide)
1. **Slide 1: The Answer** — Recommendation in one sentence. Be direct. Be specific.
   - ❌ "We've been analysing the market opportunity…"
   - ✅ "We should acquire Company X for £15M — it will generate £4M annual savings within 18 months."
2. **Slide 2: Supporting Point 1** — Strongest argument + evidence
3. **Slide 3: Supporting Point 2** — Second strongest + evidence
4. **Slide 4: Supporting Point 3** — Third argument. Never more than 3.
5. **Slide 5: Implications** — Risks, dependencies, timeline
6. **Slide 6: Next Steps** — One clear ask

### The Rule of Three
- Humans hold 3-4 items in working memory
- More than 3 points → audience forgets first one
- Fewer than 3 → argument feels thin

### MECE Rule (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)
- **Mutually Exclusive:** No overlap between points
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** Together, cover all important ground
- Test: If someone accepted all 3 points, would they accept the answer?

### 3 Common Mistakes
1. **Burying the answer in context** — State it plainly. Context comes after.
2. **More than 3 supporting points** — If you have 5, you haven't synthesized.
3. **Supporting points that don't support** — Every point must answer "why should I accept your recommendation?"

### Before & After Case Study
- **BEFORE:** 10 slides building to conclusion → CEO checked out by slide 4
- **AFTER:** 5 slides with answer first → CEO approved in meeting, 8 minutes

### When NOT to Use
- Audience needs emotional engagement first (use Hero's Journey)
- Delivering bad news requiring context
- Genuinely don't have a recommendation yet

## Source 2: Approval Packet Method (WinningPresentations.com)
**URL:** https://winningpresentations.com/approval-packet-method-executive-decision-deck/
**Published:** February 2, 2026
**Author:** Mary Beth Hazeldine

### Core Principle
> "A decision meeting is not a presentation. It's an approval process."

### What Executives Need in First 60 Seconds
1. **What are you asking me to approve?** (the exact decision)
2. **Why now?** (the stakes of doing nothing)
3. **How contained is the risk?** (what could go wrong and how you'll control it)

If deck makes them hunt → "This is interesting… send it around… let's revisit."

### The 6-Slide Structure
1. **The Decision** — The exact "yes" you need (one sentence)
2. **The Stakes** — What changes if we do nothing (consequence, not drama)
3. **The Options** — 2-3 viable paths (including "do nothing")
4. **The Recommendation** — Your choice + the trade-off it wins
5. **The Risk Box** — Top risks + mitigations + kill switch trigger
6. **The 30-Day Plan** — What happens next if approved (who/when/what)

### Risk Box Format
- **Risk:** plain-language statement (no jargon)
- **Likelihood / Impact:** low / medium / high
- **Mitigation:** what you will do proactively
- **Kill switch:** the trigger that stops the initiative before it becomes expensive

### The 20-Minute Build Workflow
1. Write the Decision sentence first — if you can't, you're not ready
2. List the options — leaders trust people who show trade-offs
3. Draft the Risk Box — prevents last-minute pushback
4. Add the 30-day plan — make "what happens next" obvious
5. Only then add visuals and supporting numbers

### Self-Check Before Sending
1. **10-second test:** Can someone restate the decision after a skim of slide 1?
2. **Trade-off test:** Are options and consequences visible (not implied)?
3. **Risk test:** Is downside bounded with mitigations and a kill switch?
4. **Momentum test:** Is the 30-day next step obvious if approved?

> "If you pass all four, you're no longer 'presenting.' You're making a decision easy."
