# Pilot Program Evaluation for Franchise/Multi-Unit Businesses — Raw Findings
**Primary Source:** https://govfacts.org/accountability-ethics/efficiency/evidence-based-policy/how-government-pilots-and-scales-programs/
**Published:** June 1, 2025 (Updated December 5, 2025)
**Cross-referenced with:** GAO standards, OMB guidance, HBR pilot scaling, McKinsey AI deployment data

## Pilot Program Definition
"A small-scale, preliminary trial of a new idea, project, technology, or process before it's rolled out widely. Think of it as a test drive to gather data, identify potential challenges, and optimize an approach before making a larger, more resource-intensive commitment."

## Key Features of Effective Pilots
1. **Limited scope** — Specific area, select group, or particular aspect
2. **Defined duration** — Federal standard: up to 3 years; franchise typical: 30-90 days
3. **Specific, measurable objectives** — SMART criteria
4. **Controlled environment** — More intensive monitoring than full-scale
5. **Focus on feasibility and effectiveness** — Can this be done? Does it work?

## When to Pilot (vs. Full-Scale)
- **Highly complex or novel** initiatives with unknown variables
- **High-risk or high-cost** where failure cost is significant
- **Complex problems** with uncertain solutions
- **Need for regulatory evaluation** or process innovation

## Decision Matrix: Pilot vs. Full-Scale

| Factor | Favors Pilot | Favors Full-Scale |
|--------|-------------|------------------|
| Risk Level | High uncertainty, potential for negative consequences | Low risk, well-understood solution |
| Complexity | Highly complex, new tech, many unknowns | Simple, established approach |
| Urgency | Time available for testing | Critical need for immediate solution |
| Cost of Failure | High, potentially catastrophic | Low to moderate |
| Available Evidence | Limited existing evidence | Substantial evidence from similar programs |
| Resources | Limited for full launch | Sufficient for full deployment |
| Need for Learning | High — must iterate and adapt | Low — solution is well-defined |
| Stakeholder Readiness | Need to build buy-in | Strong buy-in exists |

## Pilot Best Practices
1. **Define clear objectives and success metrics** (SMART goals)
2. **Ensure representative participation** (pilot site reflects real conditions)
3. **Robust evaluation and data-driven learning** (framework for data collection + analysis)
4. **Engage stakeholders and communicate effectively** (build ownership)
5. **Design for scalability** — "Begin with the end in mind"

## Post-Pilot Decision Framework

### Three Paths
1. **Go (Scale Up)** — Positive results, feasibility confirmed, benefits outweigh costs
2. **No-Go (Abandon)** — Ineffective, unfeasible, too costly, negative consequences
3. **Adapt (Modify)** — Mixed results, shows promise but needs improvement

## Scaling Strategies
1. **Scaling roadmap** — Where, when, in what order
2. **Resource mobilization** — Financial, human, technological increases
3. **Policy/regulatory changes** — Address barriers found in pilot
4. **Change management** — Proactive strategies for resistance at scale
5. **Governance framework** — Clear decision-making and oversight

## "Pilot Purgatory" — The Critical Risk

### Definition
When promising pilots stall and fail to achieve broader impact despite initial success.

### Statistics
- **McKinsey:** 92% of businesses plan to increase AI investments, but only 1% have fully mature deployments
- **Forbes:** ~90% of generative AI pilots fail to reach full production

### Causes
1. Technical challenges integrating with legacy systems
2. Loss of sustained leadership (initial champions move on)
3. Unclear business case for full-scale ROI
4. No structured scaling roadmap or dedicated resources
5. Organizational culture doesn't embrace change

### Prevention
- Secure and maintain strong leadership champions
- Focus pilots on high-impact, scalable use cases
- Build cultures that embrace change proactively
- Scale in manageable incremental stages
- Establish robust governance for scaled initiatives

## Real-World Examples

### Success: WIC Program
- Started as 2-year pilot project in 1972
- Made permanent in 1974
- **Key factor:** Early data demonstrating cost-effectiveness was crucial for scaling decision

### Success: Head Start Program
- Began as 8-week demonstration project in 1965
- Now serves nearly 1 million children annually
- **Key factor:** Comprehensive scope from the beginning enabled clear impact measurement

### Failure: Healthcare.gov
- Full-scale rollout without adequate pilot testing
- System capacity problems, software coding errors, limited functionality
- **Root cause:** Inadequate application of systems development best practices
- **Lesson:** Even well-funded full-scale launches fail without proper piloting

### Partial: SBA Community Navigator Pilot
- Clear objectives and data gathering strategy
- BUT didn't plan to evaluate program outcomes
- **Lesson:** Data collection without evaluation planning wastes the pilot opportunity
