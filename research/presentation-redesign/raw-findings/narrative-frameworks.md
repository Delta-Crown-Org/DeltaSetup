# Raw Findings: Executive Presentation Narrative Frameworks

## Framework 1: McKinsey Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR)

### Origin & Authority
- Created by **Barbara Minto** at McKinsey & Company in the 1960s
- Documented in "The Minto Pyramid Principle" (1987, updated 2009)
- Standard communication framework at McKinsey, BCG, Bain, and most strategy consultancies
- Also known as SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer) variant

### Core Components

**Situation:** The framing of important, recent context the audience already knows and accepts as fact.

**Complication:** The reason the situation requires action. NOT necessarily a "problem" — it's whatever creates urgency.

**Resolution:** The action required to solve a problem (or capture an opportunity).

### Critical Insight: Placement Depends on Audience Knowledge

The SCR framework is DYNAMIC — what goes where depends on how much the audience already knows:

| If audience knows... | Situation contains | Complication contains | Resolution contains |
|---------------------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Nothing about the problem | The stable era only | Problem + root causes + projected impact | Plan to solve |
| The problem exists | Stable era + problem | Root causes + projected impact | Plan to solve |
| The root causes | Stable era + problem + causes | Projected impact if unaddressed | Plan to solve |
| That a solution was attempted | Brief recap of everything | Result of the attempt | Plan for remaining issues |

**For Delta Crown:** The audience (executives/investors) likely knows the luxury hair extensions market exists but may not know the specific competitive gap or opportunity. **Place the market opportunity in the Situation, the competitive threat/gap in the Complication.**

### Ordering: S-C-R vs R-S-C

- **S-C-R:** Use when you need to build the case before revealing the recommendation. Best when the audience is unlikely to immediately accept the resolution.
- **R-S-C (BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front):** Use when the audience will mostly accept the resolution but needs supporting evidence. Lead with the recommendation, then explain why.

**For Delta Crown:** If pitching to potential investors → S-C-R (build conviction). If presenting to existing partners/board → R-S-C (they trust you, just need the data).

### Real McKinsey Examples

**USPS Case:**
- S: USPS is experiencing unprecedented losses due to (a) volume declines (b) increased retiree health benefit costs (c) lower than expected cost savings
- C: If USPS does not act, losses will worsen due to (a) e-diversion accelerating volume decline (b) competition capping prices (c) legal/regulatory requirements
- R: Four actions: (1) Drive revenue through premium services (2) Increase productivity (3) Pursue legislative reform (4) Reduce costs by cutting delivery days

**Global Steel Industry:**
- S: Industry is not financially sustainable (negative cash flows, increasing debt, deteriorating EBITDA)
- C: EBITDA margin range will remain lower than historical
- R: Restructuring required via (a) capacity reduction (b) product differentiation

### Resolution Best Practices
A strong resolution includes:
1. **What** needs to be done
2. **How** it will be done
3. **When** it will be done
4. **By whom** it will be done
5. Concrete **milestones** for measuring progress
6. **Side-benefits** and expected consequences (with containment plans)

### MECE Principle
Resolutions should be discovered by enumerating then prioritizing **Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive** options. Even if you only present one resolution, demonstrate you've considered alternatives.

## Framework 2: Minto Pyramid Principle

### Structure
```
         ┌─────────────┐
         │  CONCLUSION  │  ← Lead with this
         └──────┬───────┘
                │
    ┌───────────┼───────────┐
    ▼           ▼           ▼
┌───────┐ ┌───────┐ ┌───────┐
│Key Pt 1│ │Key Pt 2│ │Key Pt 3│  ← Supporting arguments
└───┬───┘ └───┬───┘ └───┬───┘
    │         │         │
    ▼         ▼         ▼
 Details   Details   Details    ← Evidence, data, proof
```

### Key Principles
1. **Start with the conclusion** — BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)
2. **Provide key points** — Short summaries explaining the "why"
3. **Support with details** — Facts, evidence, numbers, results
4. The busier the audience, the more likely they skip the detail layer
5. Sometimes key arguments alone are sufficient — skip details if unnecessary

### Relationship to SCR
- The **Pyramid Principle** is the LOGIC structure (how to organize arguments)
- **SCR** is the NARRATIVE wrapper (how to tell the story)
- SCR provides the emotional arc; Pyramid provides the logical rigor
- In practice: Use SCR to frame the story, use Pyramid to structure each section internally

## Framework 3: Duarte's Presentation Sparkline™

### Origin
- Developed by Nancy Duarte, CEO of Duarte Inc.
- Documented in "Resonate: Present Visual Stories that Transform Audiences" (2010)
- Validated by analyzing Steve Jobs' 2007 iPhone launch and MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech

### Core Concept: "What Is" vs "What Could Be"

The Sparkline alternates between the current reality and the envisioned future:

```
  "What Could Be" ─── ●           ●           ●
                     / \         / \         / \
                    /   \       /   \       /   \
  "What Is" ──── ●       ●   ●       ●   ●     ●──→ NEW BLISS
                           \ /         \ /
                            ●           ●
  
  Beginning                Middle               End
```

### Why It Works
- **Reports** (all "what is") are boring — just facts with a tiny recommendation at the end
- **Pitches** (all "what could be") sound too good to be true — no grounding in reality
- **Sparkline** creates CONTRAST — the back-and-forth keeps the audience engaged

### Seven Storytelling Techniques from Duarte

1. **The Structure: Hero's Journey** — Beginning, middle, end. Problem → mentor provides tools → resolution. Must have moment of realization, moment of solution, moment of "happily ever after."

2. **Audience = The Hero** — Position the audience (investor, executive) as the hero who has the challenge. Don't make your company the hero.

3. **Presenter = The Mentor** — You provide the tools/solutions. You're not seeking glory — you're empowering the hero.

4. **Contrast via Sparkline** — Toggle between "what is" and "what could be." Replace "and" with "but then" to create narrative tension.

5. **S.T.A.R. Moments** — "Something They'll Always Remember." One unforgettable, dramatic moment. Examples:
   - Al Gore riding a lift alongside rising CO2 data in "An Inconvenient Truth"
   - Steve Jobs sliding the MacBook Air into a manila envelope to demonstrate thinness

6. **Data Storytelling** — Three data techniques:
   - Marvel at magnitude
   - Humanize the data
   - Surprise with the unexpected

7. **The New Bliss** — End with a real, attainable vision of the transformed future. Not a fairytale — a concrete "happily ever after" grounded in your solutions.

## Framework 4: Hero's Journey (Business Adaptation)

### Classic Structure (Joseph Campbell, adapted by Duarte)
```
1. ORDINARY WORLD      → The market/industry as it exists today
2. CALL TO ADVENTURE   → The opportunity or disruption
3. REFUSAL OF THE CALL → Barriers, risks, why others haven't acted
4. MEETING THE MENTOR  → Your company/solution enters
5. CROSSING THRESHOLD  → Committing to the new approach
6. TESTS & ALLIES      → Market validation, early results, partnerships
7. APPROACH THE CAVE   → The biggest challenge ahead
8. ORDEAL              → The decisive moment / investment decision
9. REWARD              → The projected outcomes / ROI
10. THE RETURN         → How this transforms the hero's world
```

### Business Adaptation for C-Suite
For executives, simplify to 5 beats:
1. **Status Quo** — Where the market/company stands (Situation)
2. **Disruption** — What's changing and why it matters (Complication)
3. **The Path Forward** — Your solution/strategy (Resolution)
4. **Proof Points** — Evidence it works (Supporting data)
5. **The Transformed Future** — Vision of success (New Bliss)

## Synthesis: Hybrid Framework for Delta Crown

### Recommended 13-Slide Structure

| Slide | Framework Element | Content Type | Emotional Arc |
|-------|------------------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Title** | Brand identity, tagline | Intrigue |
| 2 | **Situation** | Market context — luxury hair extensions industry growth | Grounding |
| 3 | **Complication** (What Is) | The gap — current market problems, unmet needs | Tension |
| 4 | **Sparkline pivot** (What Could Be) | Vision — what the ideal looks like | Hope |
| 5 | **Resolution intro** | Delta Crown's solution / positioning | Confidence |
| 6 | **What Is** | Current competitive landscape, specific challenges | Contrast |
| 7 | **What Could Be** | Delta Crown advantages, differentiation | Aspiration |
| 8 | **Proof Points** | Market data, traction, metrics (Pyramid detail layer) | Credibility |
| 9 | **S.T.A.R. Moment** | One dramatic visualization or demo | Memorability |
| 10 | **What Is → What Could Be** | Risk analysis → mitigation strategy | Resolution |
| 11 | **Architecture/Plan** | Technical/operational plan (Pyramid: how/when/who) | Confidence |
| 12 | **New Bliss** | Transformed future vision — the "happily ever after" | Inspiration |
| 13 | **Call to Action** | Specific asks, timeline, next steps | Urgency |
